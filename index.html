I am a Ph.D. student in Computer Science at MIT CSAIL, advised by Prof. David Karger and a member of the Haystack Group and UID Group at MIT.

I work on designing and building systems to improve discourse, collaboration, and understanding on the web, with applications to social media, news, political discourse, education, and civic engagement. I also conduct research on computational analysis of large-scale social data to understand topics in the social sciences.

My general interests are social computing, HCI, and computational social science.

Prior to MIT, I worked as a software engineer, completed a Masters in CS as a Gates Scholar at Cambridge, and a Bachelor in CS from Rutgers.


Projects

Reimagining the Mailing List

Mailing lists today have many problems ranging from oversharing, which results in spam and email fatigue, to undersharing, which results in an under-utilized or dead list. Our new mailing list system will include many features to allow users to customize their experience and interaction with a mailing list without needing to learn a whole new interface. [website] [poster]

Mailing Lists: Why Are They Still Here, What's Wrong With Them, and How Can We Fix Them? Amy X. Zhang, Mark Ackerman, David Karger. CHI '15. [pdf]

Modeling Ideology and Predicting Policy with Social Media

Social media has emerged as a prominent platform where people can express their feelings about social and political issues of our time. We study the many voices discussing an issue within a constituency and how they reflect ideology and may signal the outcome of important policy decisions. Studying Twitter language, we find evidence of moral culture wars between ideologies. We develop measures to characterize language, and we build statistical models to predict which policy changes will pass. Many of our models beat traditional survey-based predictions.

Modeling Ideology and Predicting Policy Change with Social Media: Case of Same-Sex Marriage. Amy X. Zhang, Scott Counts. CHI '15. [pdf]

Eyebrowse: Real-time interaction around webpages

Imagine if we could interactions and visits to pages on the web more like encounters and travels in real life. What if we could 'bump into' friends while surfing the web? In this social network, interactions are centered around webpages via a browser plugin. Selectively publish the websites that you visit and engage in real-time with visitors on the same page as you. Kinetic Scrolling on Mobile and Tablets

In this project, we demonstrate how scrolling on a long webpage on mobile and tablets can be kinetically enhanced so that important content on the page is more easily accessed and noticed. [video]

Analysis and visualization of media frames

Whenever an issue is discussed in a news article or blog post, the author may choose to frame the issue in a particular light through the use of specific key words surrounding the issue. This work aims to analyze framing in news and build better visualizations to more easily see the different framings that different media sources employ.

Controversy and Sentiment in Online News. Yelena Mejova, Amy X. Zhang, Nicholas Diakopoulos, Carlos Castillo. Computation + Journalism '14. [pdf]

Identifying and Analyzing Moral Evaluation Frames in Climate Change Blog Discourse. Nick Diakopoulos, Amy X. Zhang, Dag Elgesem, Andrew Salway. ICWSM '14. [pdf] [poster]

Visual Analytics of Media Frames in Online News and Blogs. Nick Diakopolous, Amy X. Zhang, and Andrew Salway. TextVis Workshop @ InfoVis '13. [pdf]

Hoodsquare: Defining Neighborhoods

This project used Foursquare check-in data to characterize physical spaces and then cluster local areas that are homogeneous in order to find the boundaries of neighborhoods. We map both the neighborhoods and hot-spots we found for various types of places at our website. [website] [slides and talk]

Hoodsquare: Modeling and Recommending Neighborhoods in Location-based Social Networks. Amy X. Zhang, Anastasios Noulas, Salvatore Scellato, and Cecilia Mascolo. SocialCom '13. [pdf]


2012

Diurnal Urban Routines on Twitter

Social media activity can often give us clues about events and when they occur. By studying the temporal patterns of keywords on Twitter, we can gather the terms that exhibit robust diurnal regularity, find out when they spike, and reason about the underlying activity. By collecting a large geotagged Twitter dataset from over 50 cities, we can also compare how the daily rhythm of one city is different from another with respect to certain activities.

On the Study of Diurnal Urban Routines on Twitter. Mor Naaman, Amy X. Zhang, Samuel Brody, and Gilad Lotan. ICWSM '12. [pdf]
